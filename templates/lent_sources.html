Where These Readings Come From

A short history of the Lenten weekday lectionary — what is Roman in origin, what is Lutheran, and how the sources were established.

Among all the seasons of the church year, Lent alone has proper readings appointed for every weekday, not only for Sundays. From Ash Wednesday through Good Friday each day has, as a rule, two lessons — an Epistle, which in Lent is almost always drawn from the Old Testament, and a Gospel. This page explains where that daily lectionary came from, and marks out plainly which parts are Roman in origin and which are Lutheran.

1. Roman in origin — the substance of the lectionary

The daily Lenten Mass framework, and with it nearly every Gospel and Old-Testament lesson in the table, is the historic Western (Roman-rite) ferial lectionary. It is not a modern construction and it is not, in its bones, Lutheran: it is the inheritance of the whole Western Church.

  • The ancient station days. Long before Lent had a daily system, Rome kept stational liturgies on Sundays, Wednesdays, and Fridays — the old fast and assembly days. This is why the Wednesday and Friday readings are the oldest layer of the whole scheme.
  • Thursdays came last. For the early centuries Rome celebrated no Mass on Thursdays in Lent; Thursday propers were only supplied in the eighth century (traditionally credited to Pope Gregory II, †731). The Lenten Thursdays are therefore the latest-added weekdays.
  • Attested in the oldest books. The system appears, essentially complete, in the Gregorian Sacramentary and in the earliest Roman lectionaries — the Würzburg Comes (7th c.) and the Comes of Murbach (8th c.) — and was codified in the Missale Romanum of 1570 (the Tridentine Missal), carried down to the 1962 Missal. The Ember-Day multiple prophecies and the readings from the Old-Testament apocrypha (Susanna, Bel and the Dragon, the Prayer of Azariah, 2 Maccabees, Sirach) are all part of this Roman inheritance.

2. Lutheran in keeping — retention and curation

The Lutheran Reformation did not invent a new lectionary; it kept the Western one. What is distinctly Lutheran here is preservation and framing:

  • The Reformers retained the inherited pericope tradition and the Latin day-names taken from the old Introits — Invocavit, Reminiscere, Oculi, Laetare, Judica — which this table uses.
  • The readings survived in the late-medieval German missals and the Lutheran church orders that grew from them. It is that specific German recension — not the later Tridentine standardization — that Lutherans carried forward.
  • The Collects, Introits, and Graduals that accompany these days come into English through the Common Service tradition — the Common Service Book (1917), The Lutheran Hymnal (1941), and the Lutheran Service Book (2006).

3. How this table was reconstructed

The specific form shown in this app is the one assembled by The Lutheran Missal project, which is cataloguing and tagging the actual manuscript and printed missals of the German Lutheran world and reconstructing the historic lectionary from them. Their published “Lenten Lections” table is the immediate source; the readings here were transcribed from its page and cross-checked reading-by-reading.

Their method is itself the best evidence for the Roman-vs-Lutheran distinction. Where the German Lutheran books agree with the wider Roman tradition, the reading is simply the common Western inheritance. Where they diverge, the divergence is documented — see the showcase case below.

5. A Roman-vs-Lutheran showcase: the Holy Week Gospels

The clearest place where Lutheran usage parts from Rome is the Passion Gospel order on Monday and Tuesday of Holy Week. {{ holy_week_note }}

In the same way, Isaiah 53 stands on Wednesday of Holy Week and Exodus 12 (the Passover lamb) on Good Friday — placements “fairly universally attested” across the German sources, though LSB and the later Roman books arrange Holy Week somewhat differently.

6. The Lutheran alternative: the Passion History

There is a second, distinctly Lutheran way of keeping the Lenten weekdays that is not a Mass lectionary at all. Rather than the daily ferial readings, Lutheran congregations have long gathered at midweek services to hear the Passion History — a harmony of the four Gospels' Passion narratives — read in continuous portions across the weeks of Lent, ending in Holy Week. The Common Service Book of 1917 prints such an arrangement (by John Caspar Mattes), divided into seven Parts. It is provided under The Passion History as the companion to this daily lectionary.

The rubrics of the Passion History

The Common Service Book prints three rubrics — directions for how the History is to be used — at the head of the text (p. 250). A rubric (from Latin ruber, “red,” because such directions were traditionally printed in red ink) is an instruction, not something read aloud. The three are:

  1. Read it through Holy Week. “The History of the Passion may be read as the Lesson at Matins or Vespers during Holy Week, beginning with the Vespers of Palm Sunday.” One Part is read at each office through the week.
  2. Or read it through Lent. “Or it may be read during Lent, and repeated during Holy Week.” This is the familiar midweek-Lenten practice — one Part at each Wednesday service. (There are, providentially, exactly seven Wednesdays from Ash Wednesday through Wednesday of Holy Week, one for each Part.) The toggle on the Passion History page switches between these two schedules.
  3. Hymns between the Parts. “Suitable hymns may be sung between the paragraphs of each Part of the History” — the congregation's response woven into the reading, in the manner of a sung Passion.

A fourth, more solemn rubric stands within Part Six, at the death of our Lord: “At the words, He bowed his head, and gave up the ghost, all may kneel and silently say the Lord's Prayer or other suitable prayers.” This momentary kneeling in silence at the death of Christ is an old and widely-kept ceremony (the Roman rite prescribes a like genuflection at the same point in the Good Friday Passion); the Common Service Book keeps it as a permission — may kneel — in its characteristically unforced way.
